Assessment

Socrative

A freemium service that allows you to quickly create assessments with a variety of question types, including math equations, and including video/images as well. You get instant access to sores from students and can connect with your google drive for easy exporting. Socrative does have a game mode similar to other resources where students can either team up or compete individually.

PollEverywhere

A freemium polling service that allows respondents to respond to polls anonymously on the free tier, or non-anonymously on a paid tier. You can respond via text, twitter, or the web depending on how the poll is set up. A downside is that items are not graded like socrative, but does offer a variety of presentation formats for results depending on what you would like. A huge plus is an integration with Google Slides, so you can launch a poll from a presentation without leaving it.

Google Forms

Forms has mostly been used to collect feedback but at the beginning of the 2017-2018 school year it included Quizzes, which quickly let teachers collect assessment information from students and does include an auto-grading feature that makes it easy to see where students are. A downside is that it doesn’t include a game mode like other services, but as a G Suite core service, it does “play well” with other google services and makes it easy to export spreadsheets to your drive. Another advantage is the inclusion with Google Classroom.

Kahoot!

Kahoot is another service where you can ask a variety of multiple choice answers, but is chiefly a game mode. The music and colors can be a bit distracting, so be prepared the first time it is used with students for them to be distracted and will need so front-loading to get ready. A huge advantage is the ability to click on answers by color or symbol for younger students, who may not be able to read yet. If they know their shapes or colors they can respond that way. Personally, I recommend Kahoot! For younger grades and then a more mature service for older students that also may have game elements.

Plickers

An ingenious app/utility that allows you to collect responses from an audience by holding up a card in a particular direction. It is quick, easy and intuitive to use. A downside is that for multiple classes it is easiest to duplicate questions, which can get clicky when trying to separate polls. A large advantage is the only technology required is in the hands of the teacher. It is Android and iOS compatible, but know the last time I checked the phone/iPad had to be held vertically to mark questions correctly, a horizontal hold, will mark answers incorrectly because of how the cards are held by participants. Another advantage is that as the poll maker you can see individual responses, but each card held up by participants is slightly different, so it would be very challenging to tell what another participant answered without looking very closely at their card.

Collaboration

Padlet

Padlet has apps that work across different device platforms which works well for schools who both are and aren’t a 1:1 environment. Padlet works as a virtual bulletin board where users can post information in the form of text, images, links, audio recordings, and others. What is nice is the teacher can “authorize” posts before they become public and can enable the moderation of a board, so posts that either don’t relate to the discussion or have other issues can be removed. I’ve used padlet as an entry ticket and is quick and easy for students to post to, even if they haven’t done so before. Padlet can be used as a good way to collaborate on a project as an idea wall, or to spark discussion or interest in a topic. It is also a good way for students to pool their minds and get easy access to information others have found.

Connected Classrooms

Google Connected Classrooms is a community of educators who work to find connections between each other for different purposes. At the Elementary Level it is a good platform to find another classroom for a “Mystery Number” activity, or to form connections with educators across the world. The community is dedicated to connecting educators from all different places on the globe.

Today's Meet

Today’s meet is a good forum through which to ask questions about a meeting or topics for a class session. It is a paid service and there are more features on higher tiered levels than on the free level, but is a good way to quickly get ideas out into the room or provide a forum to ask questions asynchronously during a lesson without breaking stride during teaching.

Work production

Prezi*

Many educators are already familiar with Prezi, and it is a neat presentation service that makes it easy to add interesting transitions and points of emphasis within a presentation. My personal hesitation with using Prezi is that very often more time is spent looking at transitions that don’t add a whole lot to the content or substance of a presentation, rather than looking at ways the flow of the Prezi enhances the content. More often than not, the Prezi can draw attention away from the content of the presentation, or is used as a crutch to support a presentation, rather than one that enhances it. That said, done the right way, Prezi can bring powerful transitions and points of emphasis to drive home a point, the speaker just needs to know what that point is first.

WeVideo

Another freemium service, but one that allows for video editing, and so long as you are comfortable with a small watermark on the page, it does let you do interesting things with video on a tight or non-existent budget. What is a huge plus is that it works on Chromebooks, albeit a bit slower than on a desktop. This lets students get into the finer aspects of video editing while keeping a schools budget small. They do also offer school wide site plans, in addition to individual licenses. Our school has used this tool multiple times at different levels, and the inclusion of both an “easy” and “advanced” mode, gives entry points for students at all levels.

Screencastify

Screencastify is another in a suite of options available as chrome extensions that allow you to capture your desktop and record screencasts. As a tiered service, the lowest tier allows you to record screen casts of up to 10 minutes. The service does integrate with your Google Drive, making it easy to record information for later use, and a 10 minute max is enough for nearly all purposes. This utility does work on Chromebooks, giving it a leg up on other services that only work on Mac/Windows/Linux PC’s.

OBS Project

Open Broadcaster Software is an open source project with utilities for desktop on across Mac/Windows/Linux platforms. This is a very powerful tool that lets you record from multiple sources, and configured correctly, even has a green screen option. You are only limited by the space available on your hard drive, and can even stream to a variety of internet platforms. It also receives regular updates and continues to improve. There is a slight learning curve, but once you get the hang of how to configure your scenes and sources correctly, it is a powerful tool.

Book Creator - Web

Once limited to iOS only, Book Creator is now available from a Web Platform meaning it will work on Chromebooks, which is a huge step forward for the service. You can easily create books, including recording audio within the book and share it over the web. Our district has used it for students to create their own audiobooks and publish them to their parents and anyone else who would be interested. It is a fairly intuitive service and has a low entry bar. Like most services, there is a free tier that is more restricted than other plans that area available.

Tinkercad

Tinkercad is an extremely valuable and powerful tool for creating 3D models and even prototyping Arduino and other electronics/microcontroller projects. As an added bonus it is free, has tutorials and works on Chromebooks, a major plus. While it is not as powerful as other desktop based CAD programs, it should do the trick for many entry level projects. 3D models can be exported in .stl or .obj formats for printing or for Minecraft. There are many tutorial videos and starter objects for learners. I personally find the electronics hub to most valuable for prototyping and designing circuits before testing them in real life and potentially making simple mistakes that can ruin circuits or other components. It continues to grow as a service and I would highly recommend.

Chrome Extensions/Addons

LastPass

A strong password is a good password, but one problem is the stronger they are the more difficult they are to remember. Services like LastPass exist in many forms, but the premise is that by remembering 1 long difficult password as a “key” the service will store, create, and automatically re-create, passwords for other services. While I wouldn’t use it for a bank account, it does help prevent reuse of old passwords and will warn you about sites where you have previously done so.

Read & Write for Google

Up until recently this was a paid service but has now gone free. Read&Write for Google provides a wealth of tools for struggling readers, from highlighting text to providing a visual dictionary for words on websites students don’t understand. It does provide text to speech reading as well. To get a free account, teachers just need to sign up with a school email and provide some minor information about their district. There is also some support for other languages, helping students who are working to become proficient in English.

Draftback

Draftback is an extension for Chrome that works in Google Docs. It lets you review the revision history of a Google Doc, seeing in real time how it was written. The addon also lets you view statistics about the document during writing, such as where in the document writing was taking place, and how long/how many writing sessions there were. This tool is useful for students to look at their writing style and whether they are revising their work in multiple sittings. It also works as a plagiarism checker as copy/pasted code jumps into view without writing. It isn’t 100% reliable because it could be copied from another document, but does provide insight.

Ghostery

An extension similar in spirit to AdBlock, but it also lets you see who is tracking and block trackers across the internet. It is useful to know who is collecting information about you on different websites as you travel the internet.

Save to Drive

Save to Drive lets you export the contents of a website to your Google Drive in a variety of formats for later viewing/use. The only requirement is a Google Account, and the extension installed.

Pocket

Pocket is a useful link-saving resource that lets you categories the items you’ve saved. Once on a website or article you wish to save, you can click the Pocket Icon and it gets saved to your vault, where you can view it later. As a bonus, the online resource lets you view a de-cluttered version of the website, without many of the ads or extras.

Extensity

If you’re anything like me and have many different extensions installed, Extensity is another useful one. Having so many extensions open and working can slow down Chrome, and very often we don’t need all of those extension working at once. Extensity makes it easy to turn different extensions on and off without visiting the chrome://extensions tab every time you want to do so.

Translate for Docs Addon

Translate for Docs is an addon that opens a sidebar with the ability to copy/paste text into and then translate into a wide variety of different languages from around the world. It is a fairly simple tool, but does help in cases where translating the entire document isn’t necessary or required.

Lucidchart

One area Google Docs falls short on is the inclusion of flowcharts and other similar features. To alleviate this, I use either Draw.Io or Lucidchart as addons to import flowcharts into docs from an external service. While it would be great if this were a part of Docs natively, it does work as some alternatives, and gives one less reason to not use Google Docs. Depending on the service, there are both free and paid versions with more or less utilities.

Coding

Scratch

Scratch and Scratch Jr. are fanstasic entry level tools to the programming world. Desinged by MIT, this resource is used by Code.org on many of their own activities including the Hour of Code. As a drag and drop language there are limitations to what it can do, but makes thinking logically and programatically intuitive. There are many powerful things you can do with the service as well, and is useful for giving kids an idea of what is and is not possible.

Code Studio

This service is very similar to Scratch, but does include an App Studio where students can work to create their own model apps based on drag and drop principles. The App Studio is a level up from other drag and drop models and forms a bridge between them and the world of text-only based programming. CodeStudion provides entry points for people of many different ability levels and has activities designed to help foster growth in logic and programmatic thinking skills.

Edx

Edx was designed as a collaboration between Harvard and MIT to bring their courses to the open as MOOC. There are many different courses available in many different fields and many of those courses come straight from either universities or businesses themselves. Some courses offer Verified Certificates for a fee, and others even offer university credit depending on the level of rigor required. Those that do offer certificates or for credit often require completing activities by specific deadlines, but most courses are open for audit. I highly recommend the platform for diving into different topics on your own time.

MIT App Inventor

Looking to take block based programming to a new level from Scratch? MIT’s App Inventor gives you the ability to design Android apps using their online platform with drag and drop programming. The apps being designed can then be distributed as .apk files to Android devices. There are a variety of tutorials and community resources as well. Currently only Android devices are supported, however there is a crowd-fund available to bring the utility to iOS devices.

Other Resources

Smarty Pins
Smarty Pins is a geography based trivia game integrated with Google Maps. Using this tool you can drop pins closest to where you think the trivia event took place. Scores are awarded based on how close or far you are from the target.
Google Arts and Culture

Ever wanted to take your students to the Louvre or even to visit other famous pieces of artwork, but without the funds to do so? Google Arts and culture contains thousands of images and artwork from around the world including artwork of many different types and varieties. Each piece of art also contains information about the piece and its place in history. Artwork can be organized by color, time, theme, which helps students draw connections between different pieces as they study how art developed over time or how the artist represented their experience.

Google Street View

Google Street View has been around for a while now, but what many don’t know is that anywhere the car has gone and created 3D images where you can look around, you can also visit in Virtual Reality with Google Cardboard, or other similar tools. This is a huge advantage as it becomes possible to visit many different places around the world and see them as if you were there. As an experiment I have traveled the streets virtually of many places including a study abroad experience I had in college, and it was very profound to be there once again, and also to experience it in VR. As with other VR experiences, care should be taken for those who get motion sickness.

Google Expeditions

Similar to the Street View utility mentioned elsewhere, but specifically designed for the classroom, Expeditions are carefully curated and designed virtual field trips for students to go on with their teacher. There are hundreds of available expeditions for classrooms with cell phones and other devices. The Expeditions app works both in and out of a VR headset. Participants in the expedition connect along the same WiFi network with the leader of the expedition who guides from one step to the next. We are lucky enough to have an Expeditions Kit in our district, but other districts have put their own together by asking the community for older cell phone donations. One caution is that the phone must have a gyroscope inside of it in order to work with the Expeditions App, but otherwise most devices will work well. Make sure to download the expedition before beginning as some of them are quite large in file size. Once you have the devices and VR kits, the actual expeditions are available for free.

NewsELA

NewsELA is a fantastic service that provides tiered readings on a large variety of news topics. We all have students at different reading levels in our classrooms, but sometimes it can be challenging to find articles that match everyone’s ability and provide the same content. NewsELA does that for you. Once you find an article of interest you can adjust the reading level while not changing the substance of the article itself, matching the ability of students without compromising the content.

Wikipedia Simple English

WikiPedia is often a good place to start research, not as an only source, but as a good starting point. Sometimes the language being used can be above the reading level of our students or even ourselves. A little known tool is the option for a simple english language. On the left side of a WikiPedia article where there are options to translate to different languages, you can click an option to see more languages, and among them is simple English. Like NewsELA, this changes the reading level of the page while working to not sacrifice on the content. Simple English is not available for everything but is worth checking out as a resource.

Awesome Table

Awesome Tables makes visualizing data with Google Sheets very easy through its online utility. As it becomes more and more popular with schools and businesses around the globe, some features have become a premium service requiring payment, but for simple visualizations it is hard to beat. There is a great community and documentation helping to understand how to format a sheet once information is configured correctly. Resources are given to embed into external website as well. I would highly recommend for anyone looking to quickly visualize their data and make meaning without the tedium of number-crunching and manually configuring tables in a google sheet.

Airtable

Airtable is a combination spreadsheet and database that continues to grow in popularity. I use it in my classroom for students to continuously push updates to their projects, and for me to provide feedback. What is fantastic is there are different views for the same data. You can view data as cards, on a calendar, as Trello style tables, or as a traditional spreadsheet view. Airtable also integrates with a variety of different services to make importing data easy and intuitive. I don’t expect Airtable to remain free with the amount of services on that Tier for very long, so would recommend setting up an account if you are even slightly interested in using the tool for organization.

Pear Deck

Wouldn’t it be nice of Google Slides including polling or slide formats where you could gather formative feedback from your audience. Pear Deck integrates with Slides, to give you this option. It is an external service that can save to your Google Drive and does have both free and paid tiers. What separates Pear Deck from PollEverywhere is the inclusion of question types that let you draw a response. There is an Addon for Slides now that lets you import into a Google Slide from Pear Deck itself. The inclusion of different question types and drawing also makes it easier to use at younger grade levels than a tool like Poll Everywhere. For example, you could have kids draw a letter they are learning about and use that as a way to gather information about where they are at in letter/number recognition.
